Financial Health of Residents:
A City-Level Dashboard

The financial health of a city is closely intertwined with that of its residents. Financially healthy residents are better able to weather difficult times, are less likely to need city supports and services, and can contribute more to the local economy by supporting property, sales, and income taxes.

Our data dashboard provides a unique snapshot of residents’ financial health, including credit bureau data, to tell the story of city financial health. Recognizing that cities differ and their residents’ needs are diverse, we construct city peer groups to highlight shared challenges and promising interventions. Approaches cities can take
Cities can take a variety of steps to improve their residents’ financial health. Broad-based strategies from workforce development to affordable housing are critical in this effort and can be enhanced alongside programs and policies that focus more directly on residents’ financial health.
The following approaches are targeted to the needs, strengths, and challenges of this peer group. Residents’ needs are diverse and cities can tailor these broad strategies to their own local settings.

METRICS DEFINITIONS
  • Median credit score: Median VantageScore credit score. Subprime scores range from 300 to 600, near-prime from 601 to 660, and prime or more from 661 to 850.
  • Credit score, white areas: Median VantageScore credit score in predominantly white zip codes.
  • Credit score, nonwhite areas: Median VantageScore credit score in predominantly nonwhite zip codes.
  • Delinquent debt: Share of people with delinquent debt (60 or more days delinquent) on their credit report.
  • Median delinquent debt: Median amount of delinquent debt among those with delinquent debt on their credit report.
  • Home foreclosure: Share with a foreclosure in the last two years among those with a mortgage.
  • Housing-cost burdened, low-income: Share of households with incomes below $50,000 that pay more than 30 percent of income on housing.
  • Unbanked, metro area: Share of households without a bank account.
  • Health insurance coverage: Share of people with health insurance.
  • Received EITC, low-income: Share of tax filers with incomes below $50,000 who receive the EITC.
  • Unemployment rate: Unemployment rate for the civilians age 16 or older.
  • Labor force participation rate: Labor force participation rate age 16 or older.
  • Below 200% of federal poverty level: Share of people with family income below 200 percent of the federal poverty line.
  • Population change, 2000–15: Change in total population between 2000 and 2015.
  • Gini index of income inequality: The Gini index for households is measured on a scale of 0 to 1 with higher numbers indicating more inequality.
